Aircraft of the Pioneer Flight Museum

The Museum was established to preserve the memory of aircraft from the early days of flight, specifically dating from the years before World War II. Many of the museum's aircraft are flyable, and others are projects being restored or built to flying status.

Museum Aircraft

These aircraft are part of the Museum's collection, either on loan from private owners or property of the Museum.

Bleriot Model XI

The Bleriot Model XI is a replica built for the museum. It represents the aircraft used by Louis Bleriot to make the first flight across the English channel in 1909.


Bristol F.2

The Bristol Fighter was developed for the Royal Air Force during World War 1 as a reconnaisance and artillery spotter aircraft that would be capable of defending itself. spotter aircraft that would be capable of defending itself.


Curtiss JN-4C "Canuck"

The Canuck was a Canadian-built version of the famous Curtiss JN-4 "Jenny". The Museum's airplane is a restored original.


Curtiss-Wright CW-1 "Junior"

The Curtiss-Wright CW-1 "Junior" was a light aircraft built as an entry-level sport aircraft. The Museum's CW-1 is an original that is currently being restored.


Dormoy "Bathtub"

The Dormoy "Bathtub" was designed as a lightweight racing airplane. The Museum's copy is a reproduction that is being restored.


Fairchild 22 C7D

The Fairchild 22 was developed for the private owner as a low-cost sport aircraft. The Museum's aircraft is an original that was damaged in a hangar fire. We are restoring it.


Fokker Dr.I Triplane

The Fokker Dr.I Triplane is one of the most famous and instantly-recognizable airplanes of WWI. No originals exist; the Museum's triplane is a reproduction powered by an 80-hp LeRhone rotary engine.


Fokker D.VII

The Fokker D.VII was arguably the best fighter plane of World War I. The Museum's copy is a reproduction that will be powered by a converted DeHavilland Gypsy Queen engine once it is completed.


Luscombe 8A

Don Luscombe created the Luscombe 8 as the first light aircraft with all-metal construction. The Museum's aircraft is an original model 8A, powered by a Continental A-65 engine.


Meyers "OTW"

The Meyers OTW was Allen Meyers' entry into the primary training market, aiming for a contract with the Civilian Pilot Training Program. The Museum's OTW is an original.


Pietenpol "Sky Scout"

Bernie Pietenpol designed small aircraft intended to be built by the owners using basic tools and powered by converted automobile engines. The Pietenpol Sky Scout is a single-seat airplane intended to be powered by a Ford Model T or Model A engine. The Museum's is powered by a Model A engine.


Piper J-3 "Cub"

For decades, when people said "little airplane", the Piper J-3 Cub was what immediately came to mind. Developed as a trainer, the Cub was developed into a line of several different airplanes. The Museum's is a J-3C, powered by a Continental A-65 engine.


RAF SE5a

The Royal Aircraft Factory SE5a was one of the best fighters developed by the Allies during WWI. The Museum's SE5a is a replica, and is awaiting the resources needed to complete construction.


Rearwin 2000C "Ken-Royce"

The Rearwin 2000C Ken-Royce biplane was the first model produced by the Rearwin Aircraft company. The Museum's is one of only six that were built.


Rearwin 7000 "Sportster"

After producing several different models of aircraft, the Rearwin Aircraft company produced the Sportster in 1935 to capture some of the two-place sport/training market. Our Sportster is a 1936 model powered by a LeBlonde radial engine. It was donated to the museum by the previous owner, who restored it.


Thomas-Morse S-4C Scout

In World War One, the Thomas-Morse company developed the Scout as a single-seat pursuit aircraft. By the time it was completed, the model was obsolete, so saw use mainly as an advanced trainer. The Museum has two Scouts which are in the process of being re-covered so as to be restored to flying condition. Both are powered by 80-hp LeRhone rotary engines.


Other Aircraft

On any given day, you may find a number of other aircraft at Old Kingsbury Aerodrome. Some may be based at Old Kingsbury but not owned by the PFM, others may have dropped in just to visit the museum and workshops, and yet others may be projects being built for hire by Vintage Aviation Services, which shares the field with the PFM.
